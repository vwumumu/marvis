# As We May Think — Summary

## Key Takeaways

- [[vannevar-bush|Vannevar Bush]] wrote "As We May Think" in 1945 while serving as Director of the Office of Scientific Research and Development (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])
- Bush argued that scientific knowledge was growing faster than humanity's ability to organize and access it, and that existing hierarchical classification systems were inadequate (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])
- He proposed the [[memex|Memex]] — a mechanized desk with microfilm storage that could store an entire personal library and allow instant retrieval (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])
- The Memex's most innovative feature was [[associative-trails|associative trails]] — user-created linked paths between documents that could be saved and shared (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])
- The essay influenced Douglas Engelbart (interactive computing, the mouse), Ted Nelson (coined "hypertext"), and Tim Berners-Lee (the World Wide Web) (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])
- The enduring insight: the value of a knowledge system lies in the connections between documents, not just the documents themselves (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])

## Detailed Notes

Bush identified a fundamental mismatch between how knowledge is organized (hierarchically, alphabetically) and how the human mind works (by association — one idea triggers the next through a web of connections). His Memex was designed to mirror this associative thinking by letting users create personal trails through their document collections (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]]).

The essay's key limitation was that Bush placed the burden of creating and maintaining connections on the individual user, which limited the Memex's practicality. Modern approaches — including LLM-powered knowledge bases — can automate much of this connection-making, addressing the maintenance problem Bush identified but could not solve (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]]).

The essay's influence on computing history was profound: it inspired the lineage from Engelbart's NLS to Nelson's hypertext to Berners-Lee's World Wide Web. However, the web diverged from Bush's vision — it became public and decentralized rather than private and personal (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]]).
