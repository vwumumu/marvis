# Memex

## Definition

The Memex (a portmanteau of "memory" and "index") is a hypothetical device proposed by [[vannevar-bush|Vannevar Bush]] in 1945 — a mechanized desk equipped with microfilm storage, a keyboard, and projection screens that could store an individual's entire personal library: books, records, correspondence, notes, and communications (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]]). Bush's vision was closer to a private, actively curated knowledge base than to what the public web eventually became: the connections between documents were meant to be as valuable as the documents themselves (source: [[llm-wiki-concept|LLM Wiki]]).

## Key Points

- Proposed in Bush's 1945 essay "As We May Think" (source: [[llm-wiki-concept|LLM Wiki]])
- Envisioned as a private, personal knowledge device — not a public network (source: [[llm-wiki-concept|LLM Wiki]])
- The key unsolved problem in Bush's vision was who does the maintenance — creating and updating the connections between documents required human effort that didn't scale (source: [[llm-wiki-concept|LLM Wiki]])
- The LLM Wiki pattern is a modern realization of the Memex vision: the LLM handles the maintenance that Bush couldn't automate (source: [[llm-wiki-concept|LLM Wiki]])
- The concept directly inspired [[knowledge-compounding|knowledge compounding]] — the idea that a personal knowledge base should grow richer with every piece of information added (source: [[llm-wiki-concept|LLM Wiki]])
- The Memex's most innovative feature was [[associative-trails|associative trails]] — user-created linked paths between documents that could be saved and shared with colleagues (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])
- The essay influenced Douglas Engelbart, Ted Nelson (who coined "hypertext"), and Tim Berners-Lee's development of the World Wide Web (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])
