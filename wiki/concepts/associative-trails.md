# Associative Trails

## Definition

Associative trails are user-created linked paths between documents within the [[memex|Memex]], proposed by [[vannevar-bush|Vannevar Bush]] in his 1945 essay "As We May Think." They allow a researcher to link relevant articles, notes, and references into a navigable trail that can be saved for future use or shared with colleagues (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]]).

## Key Points

- Bush argued that the human mind works by association — one idea triggers the next through a web of connections — and that knowledge systems should mirror this rather than relying on hierarchical or alphabetical ordering (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])
- Associative trails were personal and curated: each user's [[memex|Memex]] would contain their own web of connections reflecting their interests and research (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])
- The concept directly prefigured hypertext, a term coined by Ted Nelson in the 1960s explicitly building on Bush's vision (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])
- Douglas Engelbart cited Bush's essay as a key inspiration for interactive computing, and Tim Berners-Lee acknowledged its influence on the World Wide Web (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])
- The limitation of associative trails was that creating and maintaining them required manual effort from the user — a problem that LLM-powered systems can now automate (source: [[as-we-may-think-summary|As We May Think]])
