# As We May Think — Summary

A summary of Vannevar Bush's landmark essay "As We May Think," originally published in The Atlantic Monthly, July 1945.

## Context

Vannevar Bush wrote "As We May Think" while serving as Director of the Office of Scientific Research and Development during World War II. The essay appeared just as the war was ending, and Bush turned his attention to a peacetime challenge: the growing volume of scientific knowledge was outpacing humanity's ability to organize, access, and build upon it.

## The Problem

Bush observed that scientific research had become so specialized and prolific that no individual could keep up with the literature even in their own field. The existing systems for organizing knowledge — libraries, indexes, classification schemes — all relied on hierarchical, alphabetical, or numerical ordering. But the human mind, Bush argued, works by association: one idea triggers the next through a web of connections, not through alphabetical filing.

## The Memex

Bush's proposed solution was the Memex (a portmanteau of "memory" and "index") — a mechanized desk equipped with microfilm storage, a keyboard, and projection screens. The Memex would store an individual's entire personal library: books, records, correspondence, notes, and communications. The user could retrieve any document instantly and, crucially, create links between documents.

The most innovative feature of the Memex was what Bush called "associative trails" — user-created linked paths between documents. A researcher investigating a topic could link relevant articles, notes, and references into a trail, then save that trail for future use or share it with colleagues. These trails were personal and curated: each user's Memex would contain their own web of connections reflecting their interests and research.

## Influence

"As We May Think" became one of the most influential essays in the history of computing:

- **Douglas Engelbart** read the essay as a young engineer and later cited it as a key inspiration for his work on interactive computing, including the invention of the mouse and the development of NLS (oN-Line System).
- **Ted Nelson** coined the term "hypertext" in the 1960s, explicitly building on Bush's vision of linked documents. Nelson's Project Xanadu was a direct attempt to realize the Memex at scale.
- **Tim Berners-Lee** acknowledged Bush's influence on the development of the World Wide Web, though the web's architecture — public, decentralized, URL-based — diverged significantly from Bush's private, personal vision.

## Key Insight

The enduring insight of "As We May Think" is that the value of a knowledge system lies not just in the documents it contains, but in the connections between them. Bush understood that creating and maintaining these connections was the hard problem — harder than storage, harder than retrieval. His Memex placed the burden of connection-making on the individual user, which limited its practicality. Modern approaches — including LLM-powered knowledge bases — can automate much of this connection-making, finally addressing the maintenance problem that Bush identified but could not solve.
